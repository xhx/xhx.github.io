Legendre's Formula

Legendre's Formula for counting prime factors in factorials for AMC 10/12
January 1, 2024 · 3 min · Xiaohui Xie

Legendre’s Formula#

Legendre’s Formula provides a way to count the exponent of a prime $p$ in the prime factorization of $n!$.

Statement#

For a prime $p$ and positive integer $n$, the exponent of $p$ in $n!$ is:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \left\lfloor \frac{n}{p^k} \right\rfloor$$

In practice, we only need to sum until $p^k > n$, since $\left\lfloor \frac{n}{p^k} \right\rfloor = 0$ for $p^k > n$.

Examples#

Example 1: Find the exponent of 2 in 10!#

$$10! = 10 \cdot 9 \cdot 8 \cdot 7 \cdot 6 \cdot 5 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1$$

Using Legendre’s formula:

  • $\left\lfloor \frac{10}{2} \right\rfloor = 5$ (multiples of 2: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{10}{4} \right\rfloor = 2$ (multiples of 4: 4, 8)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{10}{8} \right\rfloor = 1$ (multiples of 8: 8)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{10}{16} \right\rfloor = 0$ (no multiples of 16)

Total exponent of 2: $5 + 2 + 1 = 8$

Example 2: Find the exponent of 3 in 15!#

  • $\left\lfloor \frac{15}{3} \right\rfloor = 5$ (multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, 12, 15)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{15}{9} \right\rfloor = 1$ (multiples of 9: 9)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{15}{27} \right\rfloor = 0$ (no multiples of 27)

Total exponent of 3: $5 + 1 = 6$

Applications in AMC Problems#

Finding Trailing Zeros#

Problem: How many trailing zeros does 100! have?

Solution: Trailing zeros come from factors of 10 = 2 × 5. Since there are more factors of 2 than 5, we count factors of 5:

  • $\left\lfloor \frac{100}{5} \right\rfloor = 20$ (multiples of 5)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{100}{25} \right\rfloor = 4$ (multiples of 25)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{100}{125} \right\rfloor = 0$ (no multiples of 125)

Total factors of 5: $20 + 4 = 24$

Divisibility Questions#

Problem: What’s the largest power of 7 that divides 50!?

Solution: Using Legendre’s formula:

  • $\left\lfloor \frac{50}{7} \right\rfloor = 7$ (multiples of 7: 7, 14, 21, 28, 35, 42, 49)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{50}{49} \right\rfloor = 1$ (multiples of 49: 49)
  • $\left\lfloor \frac{50}{343} \right\rfloor = 0$ (no multiples of 343)

Largest power: $7^8$

Binomial Coefficients#

Problem: Find the exponent of 2 in $\binom{20}{10}$

Solution: Use the formula $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$:

  • Exponent of 2 in 20!: $\left\lfloor \frac{20}{2} \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac{20}{4} \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac{20}{8} \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac{20}{16} \right\rfloor = 10 + 5 + 2 + 1 = 18$
  • Exponent of 2 in 10!: $\left\lfloor \frac{10}{2} \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac{10}{4} \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac{10}{8} \right\rfloor = 5 + 2 + 1 = 8$
  • Exponent of 2 in $\binom{20}{10}$: $18 - 8 - 8 = 2$

Key Properties#

  1. Finiteness: The sum is always finite since $p^k > n$ for large enough $k$
  2. Additivity: The exponent of $p$ in $ab$ is the sum of exponents in $a$ and $b$
  3. Efficiency: Much faster than computing $n!$ directly for large $n$

Common Mistakes#

  • Forgetting to include higher powers: Don’t stop at $p^1$
  • Off-by-one errors: Be careful with the floor function
  • Wrong prime: Make sure you’re counting the right prime factor

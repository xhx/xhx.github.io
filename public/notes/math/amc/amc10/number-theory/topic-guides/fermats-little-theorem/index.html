üéØ What is Fermat’s Little Theorem?#

Fermat’s Little Theorem states that if $p$ is a prime number and $\gcd(a,p) = 1$, then: $$a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$$

Corollary: For any integer $a$ and prime $p$: $$a^p \equiv a \pmod{p}$$

üîç Understanding the Theorem#

Why Does It Work?#

The theorem is based on the properties of prime numbers and modular arithmetic:

  1. Prime property: If $p$ is prime and $\gcd(a,p) = 1$, then $a$ has a multiplicative inverse modulo $p$
  2. Group theory: The non-zero elements modulo $p$ form a group under multiplication
  3. Lagrange’s theorem: The order of any element divides the order of the group

Key Conditions#

  • $p$ must be prime - The theorem doesn’t work for composite numbers
  • $\gcd(a,p) = 1$ - $a$ and $p$ must be relatively prime
  • If $\gcd(a,p) \neq 1$, then $a \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$, so $a^p \equiv a \pmod{p}$ still holds

üìä Applications in AMC Problems#

1. Finding Large Powers Modulo Primes#

Problem: Find $2^{100} \bmod 7$

Solution:

  1. Since 7 is prime and $\gcd(2,7) = 1$, Fermat’s Little Theorem applies
  2. $2^{6} \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$
  3. $2^{100} = 2^{6 \cdot 16 + 4} = (2^6)^{16} \cdot 2^4 \equiv 1^{16} \cdot 2^4 \equiv 16 \equiv 2 \pmod{7}$

Answer: $2^{100} \equiv 2 \pmod{7}$

2. Finding Modular Inverses#

Problem: Find $3^{-1} \pmod{7}$

Solution:

  1. Since 7 is prime and $\gcd(3,7) = 1$, the inverse exists
  2. By Fermat’s Little Theorem: $3^{6} \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$
  3. Multiply both sides by $3^{-1}$: $3^{5} \equiv 3^{-1} \pmod{7}$
  4. Calculate $3^5 \bmod 7$:
    • $3^2 \equiv 9 \equiv 2 \pmod{7}$
    • $3^4 \equiv 2^2 \equiv 4 \pmod{7}$
    • $3^5 \equiv 4 \cdot 3 \equiv 12 \equiv 5 \pmod{7}$

Answer: $3^{-1} \equiv 5 \pmod{7}$

3. Proving Divisibility#

Problem: Prove that $n^7 - n$ is divisible by 7 for any integer $n$

Solution:

  1. By Fermat’s Little Theorem: $n^7 \equiv n \pmod{7}$ for any integer $n$
  2. Therefore: $n^7 - n \equiv 0 \pmod{7}$
  3. This means $7 \mid (n^7 - n)$

üß† Common Problem Types#

Type 1: Find large powers modulo primes#

Strategy: Use Fermat’s Little Theorem to reduce the exponent

Example: Find $5^{100} \bmod 11$ Solution:

  1. $5^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$ (Fermat’s Little Theorem)
  2. $5^{100} = 5^{10 \cdot 10} = (5^{10})^{10} \equiv 1^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$

Type 2: Find modular inverses#

Strategy: Use Fermat’s Little Theorem to find the inverse

Example: Find $7^{-1} \pmod{13}$ Solution:

  1. $7^{12} \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ (Fermat’s Little Theorem)
  2. $7^{-1} \equiv 7^{11} \pmod{13}$
  3. Calculate $7^{11} \bmod 13$:
    • $7^2 \equiv 49 \equiv 10 \pmod{13}$
    • $7^4 \equiv 10^2 \equiv 100 \equiv 9 \pmod{13}$
    • $7^8 \equiv 9^2 \equiv 81 \equiv 3 \pmod{13}$
    • $7^{11} \equiv 7^8 \cdot 7^3 \equiv 3 \cdot 5 \equiv 15 \equiv 2 \pmod{13}$

Type 3: Prove divisibility properties#

Strategy: Use Fermat’s Little Theorem to show divisibility

Example: Prove that $n^5 - n$ is divisible by 5 for any integer $n$ Solution:

  1. By Fermat’s Little Theorem: $n^5 \equiv n \pmod{5}$ for any integer $n$
  2. Therefore: $n^5 - n \equiv 0 \pmod{5}$
  3. This means $5 \mid (n^5 - n)$

üö® Common Mistakes to Avoid#

  1. Wrong modulus: Make sure the modulus is prime
  2. Forgetting the condition: Check that $\gcd(a,p) = 1$
  3. Calculation errors: Double-check your arithmetic
  4. Sign errors: Be careful with negative numbers

Quadratic Diophantine Equations#

Solving two-variable quadratic Diophantine equations of the form:

$$x^{2}+Bxy+Cy^{2}+Dx+Ey+F=0, \qquad A,B,C,D,E,F\in\mathbb{Z}$$

The aim is to decide (i) whether integer solutions exist and (ii) how to list them all (finite or infinite).

1. Normalize and Check Easy Obstructions#

Step What to do Why
(a) Clear denominators / GCD Multiply both sides by a common factor so all coefficients are integers with $\gcd=1$. Keeps later modular work clean.
(b) Reduce mod small bases Test mod 2, 4, 3, 5, 8, 11â€¦ Often rules out entire families quickly.
(c) Look for obvious factorisation Does the LHS factor nicely? (e.g. SFFT: $xy+mx+ny = (x+n)(y+m)-mn$). Turns the job into divisorâ€“counting.

Example: $xy+4y=5x+20$ â†’ $(x-4)(y+5)=0$ after shifting terms (classic Simon).

2. Treat it as a Quadratic in One Variable#

Rewrite as

$$(\text{quadratic in }x)=0 \quad\text{or}\quad (\text{quadratic in }y)=0$$

then use the fact that a quadratic has an integer root iff its discriminant is a perfect square.

Quick Template#

$$Ax^{2} + (By+ D)x + (Cy^{2} + Ey + F)=0$$

  • Discriminant in $x$:

    $$\Delta(y)= (By+D)^{2}-4A(Cy^{2}+Ey+F)$$

  • Require $\Delta(y)=k^{2}$ for some integer $k$.

  • This turns the problem into another quadratic Diophantine equation in $y,k$ but without the $xy$ term, which is usually easier.

Example: $x^2-5y^2=1$: discriminant in $x$ is $20y^2+4$.

Setting $20y^2+4=k^2$ produces a Pell-type curve.

3. Complete the Square and Rotate if Necessary#

If an $xy$-term remains after step 2, diagonalize the quadratic part.

  1. Compute the discriminant $\Delta=B^{2}-4AC$.
  2. If $|\Delta|=1,2,3,\dots$ isn’t a perfect square, perform the substitution

$$x = u\cos\theta - v\sin\theta, \quad y = u\sin\theta + v\cos\theta$$

(integral matrix with determinant Â±1) that removes $uv$.

This reduces the curve to a generalized Pell form

$$a u^{2}-b v^{2}=N$$

Standard Pell machinery (continued fractions, fundamental solutions) then gives an infinite parametrization or shows that none exist.

4. Bounding & Descent for Positive-Definite Forms#

When $A>0, C>0, \Delta<0$, the quadratic part is positive-definite:

$$Ax^{2}+Bxy+Cy^{2} \ge \lambda (x^{2}+y^{2})$$

for some $\lambda>0$. Then

$$|Dx+Ey+F| \le \text{constant}$$

forces $|x|,|y|$ below a modest bound, converting the search to a finite brute-force scan.

Tiny “infinite-descent” tweaks (iterate the equation to build smaller solutions) can finish off the last stubborn cases.

5. Summary Flowchart#

  1. Factor? â‡’ divisor-count.
  2. Quadratic-in-one-variable? â‡’ perfect-square discriminant.
  3. Mixed term left? â‡’ rotate/complete square â‡’ Pell-type.
  4. Positive-definite? â‡’ bound variables â‡’ finite scan.
  5. Otherwise use descent, modular refinements, or parametrization tricks (e.g. Vieta jumping for $x^{2}-Dy^{2}=N$).

Mini-Example Using the Template#

Solve in integers:

$$x^{2}-3xy+2y^{2}=1$$

(i) Treat as quadratic in $x$:

$$x^{2}-(3y)x+(2y^{2}-1)=0$$

Discriminant $\Delta(y)=9y^{2}-4(2y^{2}-1)=y^{2}+4$.

Set $y^{2}+4=k^{2}$.

This is a Pythagorean-triple condition. Write

$$k^{2}-y^{2}=4 \Longrightarrow (k-y)(k+y)=4$$

Because 4 has factor pairs $(1,4),(2,2),(4,1)$ (and their negatives), we get the small list $y=\pm1, 0$.

Plugging each into the quadratic gives the six integer solutions

$$(x,y)=(1,0), (-1,0), (2,1), (1,1), (-2,-1), (-1,-1)$$

All follow directly from the perfect-square discriminant trickâ€”no Pell curve required here.

Take-away#

Most AMC/AIME-level quadratic Diophantine problems break under one of three hammers:

  1. Factor to a rectangle (Simon’s trick).
  2. Quadratic-in-one-variable â†’ perfect-square discriminant.
  3. Complete square â†’ Pell-type (use continued fractions or bounding to finish).

Keep these three pathways in mind and you can tackle almost every “mixed-quadratic Diophantine” that contest writers throw at you.

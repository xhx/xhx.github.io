Modular Arithmetic#

Definition#

For integers $a$, $b$, and positive integer $m$, we say $a \equiv b \pmod{m}$ if $m$ divides $(a - b)$.

Basic Properties#

Reflexive, Symmetric, and Transitive#

  • $a \equiv a \pmod{m}$ (reflexive)
  • If $a \equiv b \pmod{m}$, then $b \equiv a \pmod{m}$ (symmetric)
  • If $a \equiv b \pmod{m}$ and $b \equiv c \pmod{m}$, then $a \equiv c \pmod{m}$ (transitive)

Arithmetic Operations#

  • $(a + b) \bmod m = ((a \bmod m) + (b \bmod m)) \bmod m$
  • $(a - b) \bmod m = ((a \bmod m) - (b \bmod m)) \bmod m$
  • $(a \cdot b) \bmod m = ((a \bmod m) \cdot (b \bmod m)) \bmod m$
  • $a^n \bmod m = (a \bmod m)^n \bmod m$

Modular Inverses#

If $\gcd(a,m) = 1$, then there exists an integer $b$ such that $ab \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$. We call $b$ the modular inverse of $a$ modulo $m$, denoted $a^{-1} \pmod{m}$.

Finding Modular Inverses#

Method 1: Use Extended Euclidean Algorithm Method 2: Use Fermat’s Little Theorem (if $m$ is prime)

Applications in AMC Problems#

Finding Remainders#

Example: Find the remainder when $2^{100}$ is divided by 7.

Solution:

  • Since 7 is prime and $\gcd(2,7) = 1$, Fermat’s Little Theorem applies
  • $2^6 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$
  • $2^{100} = 2^{6 \cdot 16 + 4} = (2^6)^{16} \cdot 2^4 \equiv 1^{16} \cdot 16 \equiv 2 \pmod{7}$

Solving Congruences#

Example: Solve $3x \equiv 4 \pmod{7}$

Solution:

  • Find $3^{-1} \pmod{7}$: Since $3 \cdot 5 = 15 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$, we have $3^{-1} \equiv 5 \pmod{7}$
  • Multiply both sides: $x \equiv 4 \cdot 5 \equiv 20 \equiv 6 \pmod{7}$

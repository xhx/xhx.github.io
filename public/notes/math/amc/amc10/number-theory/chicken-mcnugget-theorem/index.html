Chicken McNugget Theorem#

The Chicken McNugget Theorem (also known as the Frobenius coin problem) determines the largest monetary amount that cannot be obtained using any combination of coins of specified denominations.

Statement#

For two relatively prime positive integers $a$ and $b$, the largest integer that cannot be expressed as $ax + by$ for non-negative integers $x$ and $y$ is:

$$g(a,b) = ab - a - b$$

This is called the Frobenius number of $a$ and $b$.

Example#

If you have coins of denominations 6 and 9 cents, what’s the largest amount you cannot make?

  • $\gcd(6, 9) = 3 \neq 1$, so the theorem doesn’t apply directly
  • But if you have coins of 4 and 9 cents: $\gcd(4, 9) = 1$
  • The largest amount you cannot make is $g(4, 9) = 4 \cdot 9 - 4 - 9 = 23$ cents

Why It Works#

The theorem is based on BÃ©zout’s Identity. Since $\gcd(a,b) = 1$, there exist integers $x_0, y_0$ such that:

$$ax_0 + by_0 = 1$$

For any integer $n$, we can write:

$$n = n(ax_0 + by_0) = a(nx_0) + b(ny_0)$$

However, this might give negative coefficients. The key insight is that we can adjust by adding multiples of $ab$:

$$n = a(nx_0 + kb) + b(ny_0 - ka)$$

for appropriate $k$.

Applications in AMC Problems#

Finding Maximum Impossible Values#

Problem: What’s the largest number of McNuggets you cannot buy if they come in boxes of 6, 9, or 20?

Solution:

  • First, check if any two are relatively prime: $\gcd(6,9) = 3$, $\gcd(6,20) = 2$, $\gcd(9,20) = 1$
  • Use the theorem for 9 and 20: $g(9,20) = 9 \cdot 20 - 9 - 20 = 151$
  • Then check if 6 can help make numbers larger than 151

Counting Possible Values#

Problem: How many positive integers cannot be expressed as $7x + 11y$ for non-negative integers $x, y$?

Solution:

  • $\gcd(7,11) = 1$, so the theorem applies
  • $g(7,11) = 7 \cdot 11 - 7 - 11 = 59$
  • There are exactly $g(7,11)/2 = 29.5$ such numbers
  • Actually, there are 30 numbers (from 1 to 59, excluding those expressible)

Generalization to Three Variables#

For three relatively prime integers $a, b, c$, the Frobenius number is:

$$g(a,b,c) = \frac{1}{2}(\sqrt{abc(a+b+c)} - a - b - c)$$

This is much more complex and rarely appears in AMC problems.

Key Properties#

  1. Existence: The Frobenius number exists only when $\gcd(a,b) = 1$
  2. Uniqueness: There’s exactly one largest number that cannot be expressed
  3. Symmetry: $g(a,b) = g(b,a)$
  4. Bound: $g(a,b) \leq ab - a - b$ (equality when relatively prime)
